CKS Hospital Observership — Ward Rounds & ICU

March 2026 · CKS Hospital, Jaipur · Dr. Prakash Chandwani, Consultant Cardiologist

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

Description

In March 2026, I undertook a clinical observership at CKS Hospital in Jaipur under the supervision of Dr. Prakash Chandwani, a Consultant Cardiologist. During ward rounds, I participated in the complete patient assessment cycle: accompanying the team as they visited each patient, observing history-taking and physical examination, reviewing investigation results, and learning how clinical findings were synthesized into management decisions. I checked on all the patients during rounds, observing how Dr. Chandwani assessed their progress, adjusted medications, and communicated with families. I also spent dedicated time in the Intensive Care Unit, where I learned about haemodynamic monitoring, ABG interpretation, ventilator management, and how acute patients are stabilized and monitored. The ICU experience exposed me to the intensity of critical care: rapid assessments, real-time clinical decision-making, and the constant vigilance required to maintain patient stability.

Feelings

Walking into a functioning cardiology ward as a pre-clinical student was both exhilarating and humbling. I was acutely aware of the gulf between the medicine I had studied in lecture halls and the medicine unfolding in front of me. Watching Dr. Chandwani interact with patients — calm, direct, and deeply attentive — I felt a mixture of awe and self-awareness about how far I still have to grow. In the ICU, the gravity of acute illness was tangible; the environment demanded a composure and competence that I aspire to but do not yet possess. Rather than feeling discouraged, I found this motivating — I could see, concretely, what I was working toward.

Evaluation

The observership was a rich and constructive learning experience. During ward rounds, walking alongside the team and checking on patients gave me a practical understanding of how clinical care is sequenced: what to assess, how to gather information efficiently, and how to prioritize the most important clinical findings. Dr. Chandwani explained his clinical reasoning at key decision points, which helped me understand not just what was being done but why. In the ICU, I gained real exposure to critical care monitoring. Learning to interpret ABGs, understand ventilator settings, and recognize clinical deterioration in real time was challenging but invaluable. The ICU environment was fast-paced and intense, but observing how the team remained calm under pressure and made rapid, evidence-based decisions was deeply instructive. The direct teaching from Dr. Chandwani about specific findings and their clinical significance made the abstract concepts from lectures concrete and actionable.

Analysis

The observership made clear that patient care is not a series of discrete technical actions — it is a continuous, dynamic process of attending to the whole person. During ward rounds, checking on each patient and observing Dr. Chandwani's systematic approach to assessment revealed how clinical experience enables rapid synthesis of information into actionable decisions. I observed how he balanced clinical authority with patient autonomy: making definitive recommendations while consistently eliciting patient preferences and acknowledging family concerns. This is a nuanced balance, and one I had not fully appreciated from classroom learning.

In the ICU, I gained insight into how critically ill patients are managed moment-to-moment. Learning to read ABGs, interpret vital sign trends, and recognize subtle signs of deterioration transformed abstract physiology into practical clinical thinking. The ICU experience highlighted how rapidly clinical situations can evolve and how dependent good outcomes are on clear team communication, anticipatory thinking, and the ability to manage potential deterioration before it becomes a crisis. The surgeons during operative procedures explained their steps and the underlying anatomy; similarly, in the ICU and ward, understanding the "why" behind each clinical decision deepened my learning far beyond passive observation.

I also became aware of my own limitations: I found it challenging to identify relevant physical findings in real time, to remember the significance of ABG values without conscious recall, and to understand the full clinical context that senior clinicians process intuitively. This awareness points clearly to competencies I need to actively develop through continued clinical exposure and deliberate practice.

Conclusion

Clinical observership — even at an early stage — is irreplaceable as a form of learning. The wards and ICU revealed dimensions of patient care that no lecture or textbook can convey: the emotional texture of a difficult conversation, the physical toll of acute illness visible on a patient's face, and the quiet professionalism of a well-functioning healthcare team. I am more motivated than before to become a clinician who is not only technically competent but genuinely present for patients in their most vulnerable moments.

Action Plan

I will seek further observership opportunities during term breaks and summer periods. I will study evidence-based frameworks for patient communication — including the SPIKES protocol for breaking bad news and the teach-back method for health literacy. I will practise history-taking and physical examination skills in simulation settings to build speed and precision. During clinical rotations, I will proactively engage with patients in supervised, low-stakes activities — patient education, chart review, structured interviews — to steadily build confidence and competence in patient-facing roles.

Self-Reflection

Competency Reflection

Reflection — Patient Care Provider & Health Advocate

This observership deepened my understanding of what it means to place the patient at the centre of care. Patient-centred practice, I learned through observation, is not simply about conducting a thorough assessment — it is about the quality of presence and communication that accompanies every clinical interaction. I observed how Dr. Chandwani consistently positioned the patient as the primary stakeholder in every decision: not just informing patients about their care but genuinely listening, adjusting communication style to individual health literacy levels, and ensuring that the patient's values and concerns were incorporated into the management plan.

The ICU exposure specifically illuminated the advocacy dimension of this competency. In critical care, patients often cannot speak for themselves. Their safety, dignity, and best interests must be actively protected by the clinical team. I witnessed how clear documentation, consistent family communication, and robust escalation protocols serve as advocacy tools — ensuring that patient wishes and welfare remain central even in the most acute and resource-intensive environments.

I recognise that at this stage of my training, I am an observer, not yet a participant. But this observership has given me a clear and concrete picture of the clinician I want to become: one who combines clinical excellence with genuine compassion, who communicates with clarity and empathy, and who understands that advocating for patients is not a secondary duty but the very heart of medical practice. This will guide how I engage with every subsequent clinical encounter throughout my training.
