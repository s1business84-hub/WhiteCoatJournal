Reflection · Patient Care Provider & Health Advocate

CKS Hospital Observership — Surgical Theatre

March 2026 · CKS Hospital, Jaipur · Dr. Prakash Chandwani, Consultant Cardiologist

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

Description

As part of my clinical observership at CKS Hospital in Jaipur, I attended surgical theatre sessions under the guidance of Dr. Prakash Chandwani, a Consultant Cardiologist. During theatre, I observed four distinct operative procedures: tympanoade membrane graft of the ear, knee replacement surgery, prostate enlargement resection, and vocal cord biopsy. For each procedure, the surgical team walked me through the operative steps, surgical anatomy, and the rationale behind each technical decision. I observed patient positioning, draping techniques, instrumentation, tissue handling, and closure methods — gaining insight into how theoretical anatomical knowledge translates into surgical action. The surgeons explained the steps as they worked, describing the anatomical layers they were traversing and the specific challenges of each case.

Feelings

Standing in a functioning operating theatre for the first time was extraordinary. The precision of the surgical team, the coordinated silence punctuated by specific, purposeful communication, and the visible vulnerability of an anaesthetised patient all combined to create an atmosphere unlike anything I had previously encountered. I felt simultaneously privileged and deeply responsible — aware that I was witnessing something intimate and serious, and that I was obliged to be present in a way that honoured that gravity. I also noticed a sense of clarity: in the theatre, every action has a specific purpose, and I found this directness both instructive and inspiring.

Evaluation

The theatre experience was structured and well-supervised. The team demonstrated excellent discipline around sterile technique, and the time-out safety checklist was conducted with genuine engagement rather than as a procedural formality. I particularly valued observing the anaesthetist's role — a dimension of patient care I had not previously considered in depth. The limitation of the observership was that, as a student observer, there were natural constraints on how closely I could observe fine operative detail. Nevertheless, the exposure to the overall system — how theatre teams function, how patient safety is maintained, and how care is coordinated around an unconscious patient — was invaluable.

Analysis

The theatre experience reinforced several interconnected dimensions of the Patient Care Provider competency. Observing these four different surgical specialties — otolaryngology, orthopaedics, urology, and otolaryngology — revealed the breadth of surgical practice and how anatomical knowledge becomes operational in real time. A particularly striking moment was when we began studying prostate enlargement in lectures during the observership; having witnessed the prostate resection surgery first-hand, I could connect the theoretical pathophysiology to the actual tissue planes, anatomical obstacles, and surgical strategies I had observed. The surgeon's real-time explanation of steps and anatomy transformed what could have been passive observation into active learning — I was not just watching a procedure but understanding the surgical problem-solving embedded within it.

This experience also highlighted how operative care requires the integration of technical skill with meticulous attention to patient safety: sterile technique, correct counts, safe positioning, and vigilant monitoring are not merely protocols — they are acts of patient advocacy performed collectively by the whole team. The direct instruction from the surgeons during procedures was invaluable; they explained not just what they were doing but why, which connected my emerging clinical knowledge to real surgical decision-making.

Conclusion

Surgical theatre observation demonstrated that patient care in the operative setting is a team-based, protocol-driven, and deeply human endeavour. The patient places complete trust in a team of strangers while in their most vulnerable state. Honouring that trust requires not only technical excellence but the kind of discipline, communication, and mutual accountability I observed in this team. I leave this experience with a deeper respect for the perioperative environment and a clearer sense of the standards I am working toward.

Action Plan

I will deepen my understanding of perioperative medicine — including pre-operative optimisation, intraoperative monitoring, and post-operative care pathways — through structured reading and case-based learning. During surgical rotations, I will actively seek opportunities to observe the consent process and participate in pre-operative clerking under supervision. I will also explore the role of the surgical safety checklist and the evidence base behind it, to understand how systemic approaches to safety protect patients at every step of the operative journey.

Self-Reflection

Competency Reflection

Reflection — Patient Care Provider & Health Advocate

This theatre experience expanded my understanding of patient care to encompass the perioperative domain — an environment where patient advocacy is exercised in the absence of the patient's voice. The entire theatre team acts as proxy advocates for a patient who has placed absolute trust in the system. Observing how this responsibility is operationalised — through checklists, clear role definition, sterile discipline, and continuous monitoring — deepened my appreciation for the systems-thinking that underpins safe patient care.

The experience also highlighted the breadth of the Patient Care Provider role. Patient care extends beyond the bedside conversation or the physical examination; it encompasses every decision, action, and communication that affects the patient's safety and dignity, including those made while the patient is unconscious. The anaesthetist protecting the airway, the scrub nurse tracking every instrument, the surgeon pausing to re-confirm laterality — each of these is an act of patient care, expressed through competence and vigilance rather than words.

As I continue my medical training, I am developing a philosophy of care that integrates technical competence with advocacy, presence, and professional accountability. This observership was a formative contribution to that philosophy. It showed me that being a truly patient-centred clinician requires not only clinical knowledge but the moral clarity to act consistently in the patient's best interest — especially in environments where the patient cannot represent themselves.
