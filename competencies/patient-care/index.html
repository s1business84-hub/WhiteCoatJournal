A

Description of the Activity

What happened — the factual account

From 20 to 27 March 2026, I completed a structured observership at CKS Hospital, Jaipur, under the direct supervision of Dr. Prakash Chandwani, a Consultant Cardiologist. The placement was arranged independently as part of my commitment to early clinical exposure before formal rotations begin.

Across the week, I attended morning ward rounds, patient case discussions, and ICU reviews; observed clinical assessments, investigations, and multidisciplinary patient management; and gained direct exposure to professional communication, ethical aspects of care, and hospital workflow within a tertiary cardiology setting. The observership was formally recognised with a certificate of completion issued on 27 April 2026.

B

Self-Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

Step 1 Description

From 20–27 March 2026, I completed an observership at CKS Hospital, Jaipur, under Dr. Prakash Chandwani, a Consultant Cardiologist, spanning ward rounds, ICU, and operating theatre.

Ward Rounds: I checked on all patients with the clinical team, observing how experienced clinicians synthesise clinical information into evidence-based decisions and communicate management plans with families.

ICU: I learned about haemodynamic monitoring, ABG interpretation, ventilator management, and how critically ill patients are rapidly assessed and stabilized. Understanding how vital signs and blood gas values inform clinical decision-making in acute care was both challenging and formative.

Operating Theatre: I observed four surgical procedures with real-time teaching by the surgeons: tympanoade membrane graft of the ear, knee replacement surgery, prostate enlargement resection, and vocal cord biopsy. For each procedure, the surgeons explained operative steps, surgical anatomy, and the rationale behind each decision. A particularly striking moment was the prostate enlargement resection: having studied this condition concurrently in lectures, I could immediately connect the theoretical pathophysiology to the actual tissue planes and surgical strategies I observed. The direct, real-time teaching transformed passive observation into active, integrated learning.

Step 2 Feelings

The ward was immediately engaging — I felt the textbook come alive the moment a registrar began connecting clinical signs to pathophysiology at the bedside. The ICU was humbling in a way I had not anticipated: the proximity to suffering and uncertainty was confronting, and I found myself observing not just the clinical interventions but the composure and communication skills of the senior team as they navigated difficult conversations with families. The theatre generated a quiet awe — the precision, the silence, the choreography of a functioning surgical team.

Step 3 Evaluation

The placement was exceptionally formative. Senior clinicians were welcoming and took time to answer questions, explain their reasoning, and narrate their decisions — turning every observation into a teaching moment. The breadth of environments (ward, ICU, theatre) in a single observership was invaluable for understanding how care continuity works across the hospital system. If I were to critique one aspect, it would be the lack of any structured feedback mechanism; a brief debrief at the end of each day would have helped me consolidate my learning more deliberately.

Step 4 Analysis

The observership reinforced several themes that cannot be adequately taught in a lecture hall. First, clinical reasoning is iterative — clinicians continuously refine their diagnostic hypotheses as new information emerges, and the certainty of a textbook diagnosis is rarely present in real practice. Second, patient-centred care in an ICU setting demands an entirely different skill set from ward care: the patient's voice is often absent, and advocacy, ethics, and family communication become central competencies. Third, the operating theatre made viscerally clear that technical surgical skill is only one layer of operative excellence — situational awareness, team hierarchy, and sterile discipline are equally critical to patient safety.

Step 5 Conclusion

This observership was the most significant formative experience of my pre-clinical journey. It crystallised my motivation for medicine and sharpened my understanding of what competent, compassionate patient care actually looks like in practice. It also made me more honest with myself about the distance between where I am now and where I need to be — and that honesty is itself a professional asset.

Step 6 Action Plan

I will seek further observership opportunities in different specialty settings — particularly medicine, paediatrics, and emergency — to build a broader clinical frame of reference before rotations begin. I will develop the habit of structured personal debrief after each clinical exposure: noting one thing I observed well, one thing I did not understand, and one question I intend to research before the next session. I will also actively study ICU monitoring parameters and basic surgical anatomy to better contextualise what I observed during this placement.
