Reflection · Medical Expert

Peripheral IV Cannulation

2024 · Gulf Medical University Clinical Skills Lab

Personal Reflection

A

Description of the Activity

What happened — the factual account

During my clinical skills training at Gulf Medical University, I had the opportunity to learn and practise peripheral intravenous (IV) cannulation under supervised conditions. The session covered the principles of aseptic technique, appropriate vein selection on a practice arm, correct bevel-up insertion angle, flashback confirmation, cannula advancement and securing, followed by line flushing and sterile dressing. The photograph above captures the moment of cannula insertion on the practice arm — tourniquet applied, gloved hands stabilising the vein, cannula held at the correct bevel-up angle ready for advancement.

This experience was undertaken as part of developing essential procedural competencies required in clinical practice. Engaging in this hands-on skill enabled me to translate theoretical knowledge of vascular anatomy, infection control, and patient safety into practical application — recognising the importance of technical competence in both routine and emergency patient care.

B

Self-Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

Step 1 Description

The session began with a demonstration by the instructor showing vein selection, tourniquet application, bevel-up insertion at the correct angle, and advancement of the cannula into the lumen. We then rotated through practice stations. My first attempt resulted in through-and-through puncture of the simulated vein; on the second attempt, I achieved successful placement confirmed by flashback and a patent flush. The photograph captures the active moment of insertion — tourniquet in place above the antecubital fossa, left hand stabilising the practice arm, right hand guiding the cannula at the correct approach angle.

Step 2 Feelings

I experienced an initial degree of hesitation during early attempts — particularly maintaining technical precision while managing the angle of approach and the speed of advancement. My first failed attempt created a sharper awareness of the precision this procedure demands. The success on the second attempt brought both relief and a realistic understanding: consistent competence will require many more repetitions before I am ready for clinical application on real patients.

Step 3 Evaluation

The skills lab provided an ideal environment for early-stage practice — immediate feedback, no patient risk, and the freedom to fail without consequence. Guided supervision and immediate correction facilitated a gradual improvement in both confidence and procedural accuracy. The limitation was session length: one or two attempts per student is insufficient for genuine skill consolidation. Returning to photos of the equipment also helped me review grip mechanics and sharps handling between sessions.

Step 4 Analysis

This experience highlighted that procedural competence extends beyond technical skill alone. It requires effective patient communication, patience, and strict adherence to aseptic protocols to minimise the risk of infection and procedural complications. Intravenous access is a fundamental component of patient management — a critical link between diagnosis and therapeutic intervention. A poorly sited or infected cannula is not merely uncomfortable: it is a direct route for bloodstream infection. This deepened my understanding of why phlebitis rates and line infection rates are monitored as clinical quality indicators, and why aseptic non-touch technique is non-negotiable.

Step 5 Conclusion

One session is a foundation, not competence. This experience strengthened my understanding of the responsibility associated with performing clinical procedures safely. The gap between a successful model-arm insertion and confident clinical competence in real patients is significant — and closing it requires deliberate, repeated, supervised practice and structured feedback from clinical supervisors.

Step 6 Action Plan

I will attend every available skills lab session offering cannulation practice and actively seek structured feedback to refine my technique. During hospital placements, I will observe and assist with cannulation under senior supervision, progressively taking on more of the procedure as my competence is verified. I intend to apply this skill in supervised clinical settings to build confidence while ensuring patient safety and comfort — integrating procedural competence with clinical decision-making for holistic patient management. I will also study venous anatomy in detail, particularly common variant sites, to improve vein selection judgement before my clinical years.
