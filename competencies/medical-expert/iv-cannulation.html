Reflection · Medical Expert

Peripheral IV Cannulation

Clinical Skills Training · Supervised by Dr. Sayed · Gulf Medical University

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

Description

During a supervised clinical skills session at Gulf Medical University under Dr. Sayed, I practised peripheral IV cannulation on a training manikin. The session began with a demonstration of the full procedure: selecting an appropriate vein, applying a tourniquet, cleaning the skin with chlorhexidine, inserting the cannula at the correct angle, confirming flashback, advancing the catheter, removing the needle, and securing the cannula with a sterile dressing. I then attempted the procedure several times with direct supervision and verbal guidance from Dr. Sayed.

Feelings

I was noticeably nervous at the outset. There is an inherent tension in learning a procedure that, in real clinical practice, causes a patient discomfort. I was acutely aware that even on a manikin, every hesitation or misdirected movement would translate directly to a poor experience for a real patient. My first attempt was too tentative — I hesitated at the point of skin entry, which compromised the angle. By the third attempt, with feedback from Dr. Sayed, I began to feel the subtle tactile cues that indicate successful flashback, and the procedure began to feel more controlled. There was a quiet satisfaction in that moment of improvement.

Evaluation

The session was well-organised and Dr. Sayed's feedback was specific and immediately actionable: adjust the angle, relax the grip on the cannula, maintain skin traction, watch for the flashback chamber. The training manikin, while helpful, has clear limitations — the skin does not behave like human skin, the veins do not roll, and there is no patient anxiety to manage. This meant the technical skill could be isolated and practised, but the full clinical complexity remained beyond what the simulation could offer. The session left me with a solid procedural framework but made clear that real competence will only come through supervised practice on actual patients.

Analysis

Peripheral IV cannulation is a convergence of several distinct competencies: anatomical knowledge (identifying suitable veins in the forearm, antecubital fossa, and hand), technical motor skill (maintaining the correct angle and advancement speed), aseptic discipline (not contaminating the field), and patient communication (explaining the procedure, managing anxiety, and obtaining consent). I recognised that technical proficiency degrades without regular practice — it is a procedural skill that must be maintained through repetition. I also became aware of how much patient communication shapes the procedure itself: a calm, informed patient holds their arm steady and has less vasospasm, which directly improves first-pass success.

Conclusion

Peripheral IV cannulation is a fundamental bedside skill that sits at the intersection of technical competence, aseptic discipline, and patient-centred communication. Simulation-based training provides a necessary foundation, but clinical competence requires supervised practice in real patient encounters. The experience confirmed that deliberate, reflective practice — not just repetition — is what builds lasting procedural skill.

Action Plan

I will seek supervised opportunities to perform peripheral IV cannulation in clinical settings during my rotations, beginning with observation and progressing to supervised attempts. I will review the anatomy of venous access sites and common complications (haematoma, extravasation, phlebitis) before each clinical session. I will track my attempts and reflect on each one — noting what went well, what failed, and what I will do differently — until first-pass success becomes consistent.

Self-Reflection

Competency Reflection

Reflection — Medical Expert

This clinical skills session reinforced that the Medical Expert competency is not limited to the retention of factual knowledge — it encompasses the translation of that knowledge into safe, skilled clinical action. Peripheral IV cannulation requires the integration of anatomical understanding, procedural technique, and aseptic principles, all of which must be performed while simultaneously attending to the patient's comfort and communication needs. The session made visible a gap between knowing the procedure in theory and executing it with controlled, confident precision.

The experience also highlighted the developmental nature of procedural competence. Proficiency is not achieved in a single session; it is built through cycles of practice, feedback, and deliberate reflection. Dr. Sayed's immediate corrective feedback following each attempt was essential — it prevented the reinforcement of poor technique and provided a clear corrective direction at each stage. This mode of supervised learning, where errors are identified and corrected in real time, is the model I intend to seek during all clinical rotations.

Moving forward, I recognise that my development as a Medical Expert will require not only continued procedural practice but also the cultivation of clinical judgement about when to attempt a procedure, when to seek assistance, and how to make the experience as safe and comfortable as possible for the patient. These higher-order clinical skills will grow through exposure, reflection, and ongoing supervision throughout my training.
