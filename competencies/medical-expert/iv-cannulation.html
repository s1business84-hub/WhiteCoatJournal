Peripheral IV Cannulation

2024 · Gulf Medical University Clinical Skills Lab

Personal Reflection

A

Description of the Activity

What happened — the factual account

During clinical skills training at Gulf Medical University, I practised peripheral IV cannulation under supervision. The session covered aseptic technique, vein selection, bevel-up insertion, flashback confirmation, cannula advancement, and sterile dressing. The photograph captures the moment of insertion — tourniquet applied, gloved hands stabilising the vein, cannula at the correct angle. This hands-on experience translated theoretical knowledge of vascular anatomy and infection control into practical application.

B

Self-Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

Step 1 Description

The instructor demonstrated vein selection, tourniquet application, and cannula insertion before we rotated through practice stations. My first attempt resulted in through-and-through puncture; the second achieved successful placement confirmed by flashback and a patent flush. The photograph captures this moment — tourniquet applied, left hand stabilising the arm, right hand guiding the cannula at the correct angle.

Step 2 Feelings

Initial hesitation made it difficult to maintain technical precision with angle and advancement speed simultaneously. The first failed attempt sharpened my awareness of what this procedure demands. Success on the second attempt brought relief, but also a clear understanding: consistent competence requires many more repetitions before clinical application on real patients.

Step 3 Evaluation

The skills lab provided an ideal environment — immediate feedback, no patient risk, and freedom to fail without consequence. Supervision and correction improved both confidence and accuracy. The limitation was session length: one or two attempts per student is insufficient for genuine skill consolidation.

Step 4 Analysis

Procedural competence extends beyond technical skill — it requires patient communication, patience, and strict aseptic technique. A poorly sited or infected cannula is a direct route for bloodstream infection, not merely discomfort. This deepened my understanding of why phlebitis and line infection rates are monitored as quality indicators, and why aseptic non-touch technique is non-negotiable.

Step 5 Conclusion

One session is a foundation, not competence. This experience strengthened my understanding of the responsibility associated with performing clinical procedures safely. The gap between a successful model-arm insertion and confident clinical competence in real patients is significant — and closing it requires deliberate, repeated, supervised practice and structured feedback from clinical supervisors.

Step 6 Action Plan

I will attend every available cannulation session and seek structured feedback to refine technique. During placements, I will observe and progressively assist under senior supervision. I will also study venous anatomy — particularly variant sites — to improve vein selection judgement before entering clinical years.
