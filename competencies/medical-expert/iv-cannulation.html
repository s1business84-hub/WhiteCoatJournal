Reflection · Medical Expert

IV Cannulation Training

2024 · Gulf Medical University Skills Lab
A

Description of the Activity

What happened — the factual account

I attended an IV cannulation training session at the Gulf Medical University clinical skills laboratory. The session was facilitated by a clinical skills instructor and included a briefing on the indications, contraindications, and complications of peripheral intravenous access, followed by supervised practice on phlebotomy arms. Each student performed cannula insertion, flushed the line, secured the device, and practised documentation of site, gauge, and date. The training emphasised strict aseptic technique throughout — hand hygiene, appropriate skin preparation, and sterile dressing — before and after the procedure.

B

Self-Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

Step 1 Description

The session began with a demonstration by the instructor showing vein selection, tourniquet application, bevel-up insertion at the correct angle, and advancement of the cannula into the lumen. We then rotated through practice stations. My first attempt resulted in through-and-through puncture of the simulated vein; on the second attempt, I achieved successful placement confirmed by flashback and a patent flush.

Step 2 Feelings

My first failed attempt created a sharper awareness of the precision this procedure demands — it is not simply inserting a needle. A combination of nervousness and eagerness to succeed had caused me to advance too quickly. The success on the second attempt brought both relief and a realistic understanding: consistent competence will require many more repetitions before I am ready for clinical application on real patients.

Step 3 Evaluation

The skills lab provided an ideal environment for early-stage practice — immediate feedback, no patient risk, and the freedom to fail without consequence. The instructor's real-time corrections were valuable; being told exactly where my angle deviated or how my grip was creating instability was far more effective than reading a textbook description. The limitation was session length — one or two attempts per student is insufficient for genuine skill consolidation.

Step 4 Analysis

IV cannulation is a procedural skill that sits at the intersection of technical precision, patient communication, and clinical judgement. A poorly sited or infected cannula is not merely uncomfortable — it is a direct route for bloodstream infection. This experience reinforced the non-negotiable nature of aseptic technique and brought home why phlebitis rates and line infection rates are monitored as clinical quality indicators. It also highlighted that procedural competence in medical education must be built gradually: observe → assist → perform under supervision → perform independently, with clear sign-off at each stage.

Step 5 Conclusion

One session is a foundation, not competence. I leave with a clear understanding of the correct technique and a realistic appreciation of where my skill currently sits. The gap between a successful model-arm insertion and confident clinical competence is significant — and closing it requires deliberate, repeated, supervised practice.

Step 6 Action Plan

I will attend every available skills lab session offering cannulation practice and request additional supervised time with the clinical skills team. During hospital placements, I will observe and assist with cannulation under senior supervision, progressively taking on more of the procedure as my competence is verified. I will also study venous anatomy in detail — particularly common variant sites — to improve vein selection judgement before my clinical years.
