Reflection · Medical Expert

Laboratory Visit – Thumbay Hospital

2024 · Thumbay Hospital, Ajman
A

Description of the Activity

What happened — the factual account

As part of my early clinical exposure at Gulf Medical University, I participated in an organised visit to the clinical laboratory at Thumbay Hospital in Ajman. The session provided a structured introduction to the hospital laboratory environment, covering the major departments — haematology, biochemistry, microbiology, and blood banking. A senior laboratory scientist guided the group through sample processing workflows, quality-control procedures, and how lab results feed directly into clinical decision-making. I observed specimen handling from receipt through analysis to reporting, reinforcing the importance of pre-analytical, analytical, and post-analytical phases in ensuring result accuracy.

B

Self-Reflection

Gibbs Reflective Cycle

Step 1 Description

The visit took place at Thumbay Hospital's laboratory department. A laboratory scientist walked the group through each major section, explaining the function of automated analysers, chain-of-custody procedures for specimens, and how reference ranges are established and interpreted. We observed blood film preparation, urine analysis, and the microbiology culture area, while adhering to strict infection-control protocols throughout.

Step 2 Feelings

I was genuinely fascinated by how much of clinical medicine relies on the precision of laboratory science. The scale of automation surprised me — I had imagined manual testing as far more prevalent. There was also a moment of humility when I realised how many clinical errors could originate before a sample even reaches the analyser, due to incorrect labelling or delayed transport.

Step 3 Evaluation

The visit was well-paced and gave a meaningful overview of how lab departments operate. The scientist was generous with explanations and welcomed questions. One limitation was that we observed rather than actively participated; hands-on sample handling — even in a supervised, low-risk context — would have cemented the learning more effectively. Nevertheless, seeing the equipment in operation made the pre-clinical content on clinical chemistry and haematology far more tangible.

Step 4 Analysis

Laboratories are the invisible infrastructure of clinical medicine. The experience highlighted that diagnostic accuracy is not solely the responsibility of the requesting clinician — it is a shared responsibility extending from the moment a sample is collected. Errors in sample labelling, transport conditions, or tube selection can render even the most sophisticated analyser unreliable. This reinforced the concept of the total testing process and underlined why a physician must understand what happens to a sample after they order the test, not merely how to interpret the result.

Step 5 Conclusion

This visit fundamentally changed how I read blood results in my study sessions — I now think of each value not as a number on a page but as the end product of a carefully managed chain of processes. Appreciating the laboratory as a clinical partner rather than a black box will make me a more thoughtful and accurate clinician.

Step 6 Action Plan

I will seek additional laboratory attachments during clinical placements, particularly in biochemistry and haematology, to build on this observational foundation. I will practise interpreting full blood count and metabolic panel results systematically, understanding both the analytical method and its clinical limitations. I also plan to review the CLSI guidelines on pre-analytical best practices to strengthen my understanding of correct specimen handling.
