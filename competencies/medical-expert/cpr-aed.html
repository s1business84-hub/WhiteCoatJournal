CPR & AED Certification

August 2024 · Dubai Health Authority – Hayat Institute · 272 words

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

Description

In August 2024, I completed a CPR and AED certification course run by the Dubai Health Authority in collaboration with Hayat Institute. The training combined theoretical instruction with supervised practical sessions covering adult, child, and infant resuscitation protocols, including the safe and correct use of an automated external defibrillator. I enrolled independently to strengthen my emergency-response skills beyond the standard curriculum and to ensure I could act confidently in a real cardiac arrest situation.

Feelings

Initially, I felt anxious about performing the steps correctly under timed conditions — particularly compression depth, rate, full chest recoil, and accurate AED pad placement. There was an awareness of the stakes involved; these are skills meant for real emergencies. As the session progressed, the supportive training environment and the opportunity to repeat each step gradually replaced anxiety with growing confidence and a clear sense of clinical responsibility.

Evaluation

The course was well-structured: clear demonstrations were followed by immediate, constructive feedback after each attempt. Hands-on stations made the learning tangible and memorable. Instructors consistently emphasised teamwork, role assignment, and closed-loop communication during simulated arrests — all of which I found invaluable. The main limitation was the relatively brief group practice time, which restricted how many opportunities I had to independently lead a full resuscitation scenario from recognition through to handover.

Analysis

The experience revealed that successful resuscitation depends on far more than technical proficiency. It requires calm leadership under extreme pressure, coordinated team roles, and clear closed-loop communication — dimensions that cannot be learned from textbooks alone. Understanding the physiological rationale behind adequate compression depth, minimal interruptions, full recoil, and the timing of defibrillation deepened my respect for evidence-based emergency protocols. The AED component was particularly instructive: seeing how early defibrillation transforms survival outcomes in ventricular fibrillation reinforced why every clinician must be comfortable and confident with the device.

Conclusion

I now view CPR and AED competence as a fundamental ethical responsibility of every clinician, not an optional supplement to formal training. The certification meaningfully improved both my technical skill and my psychological readiness to act decisively in a genuine emergency. It also underscored that emergency response is a team endeavour — no single clinician resuscitates a patient; a well-coordinated team does.

Action Plan

I will renew my CPR and AED certification before expiry and pursue Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ACLS) training during my clinical years. I will actively seek supervised opportunities to apply these skills in hospital settings, and I will encourage peers to maintain current certification — contributing to a safer learning and clinical environment for everyone.

Self-Reflection

Competency Reflection

Reflection — Medical Expert

The CPR/AED course provided structured instruction and supervised practice in performing chest compressions, rescue breathing, and the safe operation of an automated external defibrillator. Initially, there was anxiety about executing the procedures correctly, but the supportive and guided training environment helped build both confidence and technical competence. Through this experience, the immense responsibility of responding effectively in emergencies became clear, and the training proved highly beneficial in developing the technical and interpersonal skills required under pressure.

The experience emphasised that successful resuscitation relies not only on technical proficiency but also on teamwork, coordination, and calm communication during high-stakes situations. Understanding the physiological basis of CPR — adequate compression depth, full chest recoil, minimal interruptions, and early defibrillation — deepened my appreciation for evidence-based emergency protocols and the chain of survival. The AED component highlighted how timely defibrillation can dramatically improve outcomes in cardiac arrest, reinforcing why every clinician must be confident and competent with the device.

This certification reinforced my motivation to remain clinically prepared for real-life emergencies while highlighting the ethical obligation to maintain and regularly update essential lifesaving skills. As an MD student, I now view CPR and AED competence as a fundamental professional responsibility, not an optional extra, and I recognise that integrating these skills with sound clinical judgement and collaborative team-based care is essential to becoming the physician I want to be.
