I · Medical Expert

Medical Expert

Applies medical knowledge, clinical skills, and professional values to deliver high-quality, patient-centred care — from initial assessment through diagnosis and management.

📄 CPR & AED Certificate 🏥 CKS Cardiology Observership 🫀 Coronary Angiogram Review
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where did this occur?
During my cardiology observership at CKS Hospital in Dubai, I observed a consultant cardiologist reviewing coronary angiogram findings with a multidisciplinary team in the catheterisation laboratory. The cardiologist walked through each patient's imaging systematically, identifying blockages and discussing management options. Earlier that week I also completed my Dubai CPR and AED certification — covering basic life support, defibrillator use, and airway management. These two experiences formed my first direct exposure to applied medical expertise in a clinical environment.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
I felt a mixture of awe and intellectual humility. Watching the consultant navigate complex imaging data with apparent ease made me acutely aware of how much foundational knowledge underpins every clinical decision. I was not overwhelmed — instead I felt motivated, drawn into the precision and purpose of each action. The CPR certification made me feel immediately capable; holding the defibrillator gave me a tangible sense of readiness that classroom anatomy had not yet provided.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both the positive and negative facets of the experience.
What worked well was the structured environment: both experiences had clear objectives and I could follow the logic of each event. The consultant occasionally explained his reasoning aloud, which was invaluable. What was less effective was my own silence — I hesitated to ask questions during the angiogram review, unsure whether my curiosity was appropriate. This self-censorship meant I missed potential learning moments.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did. Utilise literature and professional expertise.
The gap between theoretical knowledge and clinical application was wider than anticipated. Medical school had equipped me with anatomical concepts, but the observership revealed how physicians synthesise imaging, history, and pathophysiology simultaneously in real time. My hesitation to speak reflected uncertainty about my role as a pre-clinical student — a boundary that need not have been so rigid. The CPR certification demonstrated that procedural competence is achievable at any stage of training.
Stage 5ConclusionDetermine what you could have done differently and what you've learned.
I should have prepared a list of focused clinical questions before attending the observership. Asking even one well-formed question would have reinforced the material and signalled genuine engagement to the team. I also recognise that passive observation is insufficient; future placements should be paired with pre-reading on the relevant conditions so I can place observations within a theoretical framework as they unfold.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan detailing what you'll do if a similar situation arises in the future.
Moving forward, I will prepare two to three specific learning objectives before each clinical encounter, review basic pathophysiology the night before, and actively seek brief debriefs with supervising clinicians. I will renew my CPR certification annually and explore advanced life support training in my second year, building the habit of intentional, structured observation throughout my training.
II · Patient Care

Patient Care Provider

Places patients at the centre of all care decisions, safeguards dignity, and advocates for equitable access to healthcare services across diverse populations.

🏥 Bedside Interaction — CKS Hospital 🫀 Interventional Cath Lab Observation
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where did this occur?
During the CKS Hospital observership, I observed a consultant cardiologist conduct a pre-procedure consultation with an elderly patient scheduled for cardiac catheterisation. The consultant spent time explaining the procedure in simple terms, repeatedly checked the patient's understanding, and addressed the family's concerns with equal care. It was the first time I witnessed patient-centred communication in a live clinical setting.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
I felt moved by the gentleness of the approach. The patient's visible relief highlighted how powerful simple human reassurance can be. The patient's complete trust left me with a deep sense of the responsibility that comes with being a physician, and I felt eager to develop the same communication skills.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both the positive and negative facets of the experience.
Positive: the consultant's use of lay language, eye contact, and active listening were deliberate and clearly learned through practice. Less positive: I had no opportunity to interact with the patient directly, highlighting the distinction between witnessing care and actually providing it.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did. Utilise literature and professional expertise.
The consultant's effectiveness stemmed from years of training. In Dubai's multicultural context — where patients come from diverse linguistic and cultural backgrounds — clear, compassionate explanation is especially critical. This reinforced that patient care extends beyond clinical procedures; the emotional and informational dimensions are equally vital to patient safety and satisfaction.
Stage 5ConclusionDetermine what you could have done differently and what you've learned.
I wish I had documented specific phrases and techniques the consultant used. A reflection journal from day one would have captured these observations. I also recognise the need to engage with patients in supervised community settings to build my own communication repertoire before clinical rotations.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan detailing what you'll do if a similar situation arises in the future.
I will keep a clinical observation journal recording patient interaction techniques I witness. I will volunteer in patient-facing community health roles and study frameworks such as motivational interviewing and teach-back methods so that I enter clinical rotations with deliberate communication tools already in my repertoire.
III · Communication

Communication Skills

Builds meaningful, trusting relationships with patients, families, and healthcare teams through clear, respectful, and empathetic communication in every clinical encounter.

🗣️ Interprofessional Team Rounds 📋 White Coat Ceremony Address
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
At GMU I recited the professional pledge at the White Coat Ceremony 2024 and, during my observership, observed structured clinical handovers where physicians transferred patient information efficiently across roles.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
At the ceremony I felt nervous then purposeful. The handovers were humbling — the economy and precision of clinical communication inspired me and made me conscious of the years of practice needed to achieve it.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both the positive and negative facets of the experience.
The ceremony provided a memorable first experience of formal professional communication. The handovers exposed a gap: I could only partially follow the clinical content, limiting my comprehension of why certain choices were made. My attentiveness and post-session notes were positives.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
Clinical communication is a practiced art. The SBAR framework used in handovers is designed to reduce errors in information transfer. My partial comprehension reflected my stage of training, not a failing of the communicators. The ceremony showed that professional public communication draws on poise and conviction — qualities cultivatable independently of clinical knowledge.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I should have studied SBAR before the observership. For future professional presentations I will seek formal feedback from peers and mentors rather than relying solely on self-assessment.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will learn SBAR and SOAP frameworks during Year 1 and practise applying them in simulated case presentations. I will join a peer communication study group and volunteer for public speaking opportunities at GMU.
IV · Collaborator

Collaborator & Innovator

Works effectively within interprofessional teams, demonstrates initiative and adaptability, and promotes shared responsibility to achieve optimal healthcare outcomes.

🤝 Multidisciplinary Team Observation 🎓 GMU Group-Based Learning
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
During my cardiology observership I witnessed an MDT conference where a cardiologist, cardiac surgeon, radiologist, and clinical nurse specialist jointly reviewed a complex patient case. At GMU I have also participated in problem-based learning groups where students collaborate to solve clinical cases.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
The MDT was energising — the collective intelligence felt genuinely greater than any single expert, shifting how I perceive individual expertise in medicine. In GMU group sessions I have occasionally felt frustrated when dynamics slow progress, but now see those moments as practice for real collaborative challenges.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
The MDT demonstrated excellent collaborative practice: each clinician was heard, dissent was expressed constructively, and the patient remained the focal point. Less effective: my silence was a missed opportunity. In GMU PBL I sometimes over-explain rather than listening first — a pattern I am working to correct.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
The MDT's effectiveness resulted from clear role differentiation and a shared commitment to evidence-based reasoning over individual hierarchy. My tendency to over-explain reflects a desire to demonstrate competence rather than co-construct understanding — a common early-stage behaviour that can undermine true collaboration.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
In future observational settings I will identify one moment to ask a respectful focused question. In group study I will practise the listen-first principle, allowing others to fully articulate ideas before contributing.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will seek leadership roles in GMU group projects to develop facilitation skills and request feedback on my collaborative behaviours. During clinical exposures I will engage actively with nurses, physiotherapists, and allied health staff to build genuine interprofessional appreciation.
V · Socially Accountable

Socially Accountable

Recognises medicine's responsibility to society and advocates for the health needs of local and global communities, particularly the underserved.

🌍 Multicultural Patient Exposure — Dubai 📚 Global Health Seminars — GMU
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
Growing up and studying in the UAE has consistently exposed me to one of the world's most ethnically diverse patient populations. During my observership I noticed patients communicating in Arabic, Urdu, Malayalam, Hindi, Tagalog, and English — often on the same ward. GMU's curriculum also addresses global health challenges including infectious disease, non-communicable disease trends, and health equity in seminars I attended in Year 1.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
I felt a strong sense of responsibility and curiosity. The patient diversity reminded me that social accountability in medicine is present in every clinical interaction where a language barrier, cultural belief, or socioeconomic constraint shapes health outcomes. The seminars made me more informed — and more concerned about whether I was doing enough.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
Positive: early exposure to a multicultural environment is an asset. Less positive: I have not yet channelled this awareness into active community engagement. I remain a learner of social accountability rather than a practitioner of it — a gap I am motivated to close.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
The UAE's health system is built for diversity — interpreter services and culturally sensitive protocols are embedded in major hospitals. Translating awareness into action requires structural opportunity. The key insight is that social accountability is a posture, not just a skill — it must be cultivated from the very beginning of training.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I should have engaged with GMU's community outreach activities earlier in first year. Volunteering at health screening camps or blood donation drives would have converted my conceptual understanding into lived practice.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will sign up for at least two community health initiatives through GMU next semester — health literacy campaigns or diabetes screening awareness — and pursue a deeper understanding of the UAE's national health agenda.
VI · Self & Profession Enhancer

Self & Profession Enhancer

Actively pursues personal and professional development through reflection, feedback, and continuous improvement — demonstrating commitment to lifelong learning.

📓 Reflective Portfolio Practice 🎓 GMU Professional Development Seminars
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
Constructing this e-portfolio — including writing these Gibbs reflections — represents my most sustained engagement with this competency. In GMU's Year 1 professional development sessions, I was introduced to reflective practice as a formal tool and asked to maintain a learning diary, revisiting it to identify patterns in my own development.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
Initially, structured reflection felt artificial. Over time, and through compiling this portfolio, I came to appreciate that the discomfort is productive — articulating what I have learned creates a concrete record of growth that informal reflection cannot match.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
The Gibbs Cycle is genuinely useful — its six stages enforce completeness and prevent intellectual shortcutting. Less positive: my early reflections were surface-level, describing events rather than interrogating them, meaning several formative experiences were under-captured.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
Shallow early reflections are normal in developing reflective practice. Medical education literature notes that students progress from descriptive to critical reflection over time, with guided feedback accelerating the process. The depth of reflection ultimately depends on willingness to be honest about uncertainty and error.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I should have started this portfolio from my first week at GMU. The White Coat Ceremony, first anatomy session, and orientation week were all rich with reflection material I didn't capture systematically.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will maintain a digital reflection log updated after each clinical contact, share selected entries with my academic supervisor, and review it termly. This portfolio will be updated at the end of each academic year with new evidence and revised reflections.
VII · Health Systems

Health Systems Proponent

Champions quality improvement, resource stewardship, and evidence-based policy to improve patient care delivery within and across healthcare systems.

🏛️ UAE Health System Orientation 📊 Hospital Administration Observation
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
During my CKS Hospital observership I became aware of how the hospital navigated resource allocation between the catheterisation lab and general wards. The consultant explained the Dubai Health Authority's regulatory framework and I observed how insurance authorisations affected the speed of treatment decisions. GMU's orientation also introduced the UAE's Vision 2031 health objectives.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
I felt surprised by how directly administrative and systemic factors shaped bedside clinical decisions. A physician's autonomy always operates within institutional, regulatory, and economic constraints. This sharpened my sense that becoming an effective physician requires understanding the system, not just the medicine.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
Positive: seeing real insurance delays and resource allocation decisions made the system tangible. The limitation was brevity — a few days is insufficient to understand the full systemic picture. I also lacked background knowledge to ask informed questions about quality improvement or patient safety.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
Healthcare systems are complex adaptive systems. The UAE's hybrid public-private insurance model creates specific pressures that differ from universal healthcare models, directly shaping clinical workflows. My task is to build sufficient health systems literacy to engage intelligently when I enter clinical training.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I should have reviewed the DHA regulatory framework and UAE National Health Strategy before the observership. A brief policy overview would have allowed more substantive questions and more meaningful connections between hospital behaviours and their systemic drivers.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will read one health policy document or quality improvement case study per semester. I will engage with any GMU electives addressing health systems or patient safety, and seek to understand the quality improvement frameworks used at every hospital I visit.
VIII · Professionalism

Professionalism

Upholds integrity, accountability, and ethical practice in all professional interactions — demonstrating respect and empathy with patients, colleagues, and the wider medical community.

🥼 White Coat Ceremony — GMU 2024 📜 Professional Pledge
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
The GMU White Coat Ceremony of 2024 was the most symbolically significant event of my medical education to date. Standing before faculty, family, and peers to receive my white coat and recite the professional pledge made the abstract concept of medical professionalism suddenly concrete — an explicit articulation of the ethical contract between a physician-in-training and society.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
I felt proud and sobered simultaneously. The white coat felt heavier than I had imagined. The experience grounded my motivation in something more durable than ambition: a sense of duty.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
The ceremony effectively established professionalism as a core identity. A constructive critique: such ceremonies can feel like a conclusion rather than a beginning — the challenge is sustaining commitment in the ordinary, less ceremonial moments of daily study and clinical interaction.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
Medical professionalism encompasses altruism, accountability, excellence, duty, and integrity. Research suggests professional identity formation is a long-term iterative process, with formal ceremonies serving as anchor points. The sustained expression of professionalism comes through consistent small choices in everyday interactions.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I could have written a private letter to myself articulating what professionalism means to me personally — a more durable reminder than the shared pledge alone. I intend to do this retrospectively and revisit it at the end of each academic year.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will write a personal professional values statement and attach it to this portfolio. I will engage with GMU ethics modules and professionalism seminars, and seek role models among senior students and clinicians whose conduct I wish to emulate.
IX · Evidence Based

Evidence Based Practitioner

Engages in lifelong learning through critical evaluation of research and translates scientific findings into clinical decision-making to advance medical knowledge.

📖 Clinical Literature Reviews — GMU 🔬 Research Methodology Module
Stage 1DescriptionWhat happened? Who was involved? When and where?
In GMU's first-year research methodology module I was introduced to the hierarchy of evidence and tasked with critically appraising a published paper on pharmacological management of acute coronary syndrome — directly relevant to my observership experience. The exercise required evaluating study design, sample size, bias, and clinical applicability.
Stage 2FeelingsCapture your emotional response to the experience.
The process was initially overwhelming — research terminology required significant background reading. Once I established a foundation, the appraisal became genuinely engaging: I began to see clinical decisions not as handed-down wisdom but as provisional conclusions from fallible evidence. This epistemological shift felt intellectually liberating.
Stage 3EvaluationEvaluate both positive and negative facets of the experience.
Positive: the connection between the paper's subject and my observership made the evidence personally relevant. Challenging: the module progressed too quickly to develop statistical literacy in depth. My final appraisal was competent but thin in statistical analysis — a recognised gap in my current skill set.
Stage 4AnalysisDig deeper into why things unfolded as they did.
Evidence-based practice requires both statistical literacy and clinical contextual knowledge — I am still in the early stages of acquiring both. Clinical exposure often precedes the knowledge needed to fully interrogate it. This is the productive tension that drives learning, and recognising the lag helps me direct self-directed study more intentionally.
Stage 5ConclusionWhat could you have done differently?
I should have built statistical literacy independently — using resources such as BMJ's Statistics Notes or free biostatistics courses — rather than relying solely on the module's pace. For future appraisals I will practise identifying the single most important limitation of each paper rather than listing them superficially.
Stage 6Action PlanDevelop a plan for future similar situations.
I will complete a biostatistics primer before Year 2 and appraise one clinical paper per month in a relevant specialty. I will subscribe to journal alerts in cardiology and general medicine, and identify student research opportunities at GMU to contribute to the evidence base.
