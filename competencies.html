I · Medical Expert

Medical Expert

Applies medical knowledge, clinical skills, and professional values to deliver high-quality, patient-centred care — from initial assessment through diagnosis and management.

📄 CPR & AED Certificate 🏥 CKS Cardiology Observership 🫀 Coronary Angiogram Review

During my cardiology observership at CKS Hospital in Dubai, I had the opportunity to observe a consultant cardiologist reviewing coronary angiogram findings with a multidisciplinary team. The session took place over a single morning in the catheterisation laboratory, where I stood alongside nurses, radiology technicians, and a senior registrar. The cardiologist walked through each patient's imaging in systematic order, identifying blockages, assessing vessel anatomy, and discussing management options ranging from conservative medication to percutaneous coronary intervention.

Earlier in the week, I had also completed my Dubai CPR and AED certification — a structured half-day course covering basic life support protocols, automated defibrillator use, and airway management. These two experiences formed the backbone of my first direct exposure to applied medical expertise in a functioning clinical environment.

I felt a mixture of awe and intellectual humility. Watching the consultant navigate complex imaging data with apparent ease made me acutely aware of how much foundational knowledge underpins every clinical decision. I was not overwhelmed — instead I felt motivated, drawn into the precision and purpose of each action. The CPR certification, by contrast, made me feel immediately capable and responsible; holding the defibrillator and running through compressions gave me a tangible sense of readiness that classroom anatomy had not yet provided.

What worked well was the structured environment: both the observership and the certification had clear objectives, and I was able to follow the logic of each event even without prior clinical experience. The consultant was approachable and occasionally explained his reasoning aloud, which was invaluable. What was less effective was my own silence — I hesitated to ask questions during the angiogram review, unsure whether my curiosity would be appropriate. This self-censorship meant I missed potential learning moments.

The gap between theoretical knowledge and clinical application was wider than I had anticipated. Medical school has equipped me with anatomical concepts, but the observership revealed how physicians synthesise imaging, patient history, and pathophysiology simultaneously in real time. My hesitation to speak reflected an uncertainty about my role as a pre-clinical student — a boundary that, in retrospect, need not have been as rigid. The CPR certification demonstrated that procedural competence is achievable at any stage of training, and that structured practice rapidly builds genuine confidence.

I should have prepared a list of focused clinical questions before attending the observership — a habit I have since developed. Asking even one well-formed question would have reinforced the material and signalled genuine engagement to the team. I also recognise that watching alone is insufficient; future observerships should be paired with pre-reading on the conditions I expect to encounter so that I can place observations within a theoretical framework as they unfold.

Moving forward, I will prepare two to three specific learning objectives before each clinical encounter, review basic pathophysiology of the relevant specialty the night before, and actively seek brief debriefs with supervising clinicians. I will renew my CPR certification annually and explore advanced life support training in my second year. Building this habit of intentional, structured observation will strengthen my development as a Medical Expert throughout my training and into my career as a physician.

II · Patient Care

Patient Care Provider

Places patients at the centre of all care decisions, safeguards dignity, and advocates for equitable access to healthcare services across diverse populations.

🏥 Bedside Interaction — CKS Hospital 🫀 Interventional Cath Lab Observation

During the CKS Hospital observership, I observed a consultant cardiologist conduct a pre-procedure consultation with a patient scheduled for a cardiac catheterisation. The patient, an elderly man accompanied by his adult daughter, appeared anxious. The consultant spent time explaining the procedure in simple terms, checked the patient's understanding repeatedly, and addressed the daughter's concerns with equal care. I watched from a respectful distance as the interaction unfolded — witnessing, for the first time in a live clinical setting, what patient-centred communication looks like in practice.

I felt moved by the gentleness with which the consultant approached the encounter. The patient's visible relief after the explanation highlighted just how powerful simple human reassurance can be. I also noticed a degree of vulnerability — the patient's trust was complete and unquestioning — and this left me with a deep sense of the responsibility that comes with being a physician. I felt eager to develop these same skills and to understand the ethical obligations that underpin patient care.

The positive element was the consultant's consistent use of lay language and non-verbal reassurance — nodding, maintaining eye contact, and pausing to listen. These behaviours were deliberate and clearly learned through practice. A less positive aspect was that I had no opportunity to interact with the patient directly, which limited my ability to experience the dynamic personally. My role remained purely observational, which, while valuable, highlighted the distinction between witnessing patient care and actually providing it.

The consultant's effectiveness in this encounter stemmed from years of communication training and clinical experience — skills that are not developed passively. The cultural context of Dubai, where patients may have varying degrees of health literacy and may come from diverse linguistic backgrounds, seemed to amplify the need for clear, compassionate explanation. This reinforced my understanding that patient care extends far beyond clinical procedures; the emotional and informational dimensions of care are equally vital to patient safety and satisfaction.

With hindsight, I wish I had documented specific phrases and techniques the consultant used — the way he structured his explanation, his choice of analogies, his use of silence. Keeping a reflection journal from day one would have captured these granular observations. I also recognise that I should engage more actively with patients in supervised community settings, such as volunteering at health fairs, to begin building my own communication repertoire before entering full clinical training.

I will begin keeping a clinical observation journal to record specific patient interaction techniques I witness. I will seek opportunities to volunteer in patient-facing community health roles and will study patient communication frameworks — including motivational interviewing and teach-back methods — during my pre-clinical years so that I enter clinical rotations with deliberate communication tools already in my repertoire.

III · Communication

Communication Skills

Builds meaningful, trusting relationships with patients, families, and healthcare teams through clear, respectful, and empathetic communication in every clinical encounter.

🗣️ Interprofessional Team Rounds 📋 White Coat Ceremony Address

At the Gulf Medical University White Coat Ceremony in 2024, I stood before faculty, peers, and family to recite the professional pledge that marks a student's formal entry into medicine. Although the moment was ceremonial, it demanded clear, composed public delivery — a form of communication I had not rehearsed in a medical context before. Separately, during my cardiology observership, I observed team morning handovers where physicians communicated patient statuses efficiently and precisely across roles, highlighting how communication changes depending on audience and urgency.

During the White Coat Ceremony I felt nervous initially, but once I began speaking the weight of the moment settled into calm purpose. The handover observations were humbling — the brevity and accuracy with which information was transferred revealed how much skill lies beneath what appears to be simple conversation. I felt inspired to become someone who communicates with that kind of economy and precision, and slightly daunted by the years of practice needed to achieve it.

The ceremony offered a genuine, memorable first experience of formal professional communication in medicine. The handovers, meanwhile, exposed a gap in my current ability: I could follow the clinical content only partially, which limited my comprehension of why certain communication choices were made. This is both an honest limitation and a clear developmental target. What worked well was my attentiveness and the notes I made afterwards, which helped consolidate what I had observed.

Clinical communication is a practiced art that develops at the intersection of knowledge and experience. The handover structure I observed — SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) — is a formal framework specifically designed to reduce errors in information transfer. My partial comprehension reflected my early stage of training rather than any failing of the communicators. The ceremony, conversely, showed that public professional communication draws on poise and conviction — qualities that can be cultivated independently of clinical knowledge.

I should have studied SBAR and other structured communication tools before the observership so that I could follow handovers more meaningfully. For future professional presentations, I will seek formal feedback from peers and mentors rather than relying solely on my own self-assessment. Building a habit of requesting structured feedback is essential for continued improvement in this domain.

I will learn the SBAR and SOAP documentation frameworks during my first year and practise applying them in simulated case presentations with classmates. I will join or form a peer study group focused on clinical communication scenarios, and will volunteer for any public speaking or patient education opportunities available through GMU to build my professional communication confidence progressively.

IV · Collaborator

Collaborator & Innovator

Works effectively within interprofessional teams, demonstrates initiative and adaptability, and promotes shared responsibility to achieve optimal healthcare outcomes.

🤝 Multidisciplinary Team Observation 🎓 GMU Group-Based Learning

During my cardiology observership, I witnessed a multidisciplinary team case conference in which a cardiologist, cardiac surgeon, radiologist, and clinical nurse specialist jointly reviewed a complex patient case. Each professional contributed knowledge from their distinct discipline, and disagreements about management approach were navigated respectfully through evidence-based discussion. Within my medical school context at GMU, I have also participated in problem-based learning groups where students collaborate to solve clinical cases — a deliberate training structure designed to mimic real interdisciplinary working.

Observing the MDT conference was energising. The collective intelligence of the group felt genuinely greater than any single expert — a realisation that shifted how I perceive individual expertise in medicine. I felt an immediate desire to contribute but recognised my knowledge base was insufficient to add clinical value at this stage. Within GMU group sessions I have occasionally felt frustrated when group dynamics slow progress, but I have come to see those moments as practice for the real collaborative challenges of medicine.

The MDT conference demonstrated excellent collaborative practice: each clinician was listened to, dissent was expressed constructively, and the patient remained the focal point throughout. Less effective was my own contribution — my silence was a missed opportunity to at least ask a clarifying question as a learner. In the GMU PBL sessions, my contribution has been more active, though I have noticed I sometimes over-explain during discussion rather than listening first — a pattern I am working to correct.

The effectiveness of the MDT I observed was the result of clear role differentiation and a shared commitment to evidence-based reasoning over individual hierarchy. My own tendency to over-explain in group settings likely reflects a desire to demonstrate competence rather than to genuinely co-construct understanding — a common early-stage learning behaviour that can undermine true collaboration. Recognising this pattern is the first step in replacing it with more effective team behaviours.

In future observational settings, I will identify one moment in each session to ask a respectful, focused question — not to show knowledge, but to deepen my understanding. In group study contexts I will practise the listen-first principle: allowing others to fully articulate ideas before building on or redirecting them. I will also read about interprofessional education models to understand the theoretical basis of effective team-based healthcare.

I will seek leadership roles in student group projects at GMU specifically to develop facilitation skills, and will request feedback on my collaborative behaviours from tutors and peers. During future clinical exposures, I will engage actively with nurses, physiotherapists, and allied health staff to understand their roles and begin building the interprofessional appreciation that effective teamwork demands.

V · Socially Accountable

Socially Accountable

Recognises medicine's responsibility to society and advocates for the health needs of local and global communities, particularly the underserved.

🌍 Multicultural Patient Exposure — Dubai 📚 Global Health Seminars — GMU

Growing up and studying in the UAE has consistently exposed me to one of the world's most ethnically diverse patient populations. During my hospital observership I noticed patients communicating in Arabic, Urdu, Malayalam, Hindi, Tagalog, and English — often within the same ward. The clinical team navigated these linguistic differences through interpreters, family members, and visual aids. Simultaneously, GMU's curriculum explicitly addresses global health challenges including infectious disease burden, non-communicable disease trends, and health systems equity in seminars I attended during my first year.

I felt a strong sense of responsibility and curiosity simultaneously. The linguistic and cultural diversity of the patient population reminded me that social accountability in medicine is not abstract — it is present in every clinical interaction where a language barrier, a cultural belief, or a socioeconomic constraint shapes health outcomes. The global health seminars made me feel more informed and more concerned: more informed about the scale of inequity, and more concerned about whether I was doing enough to prepare myself to address it meaningfully.

Positive: my early exposure to a multicultural clinical environment is an asset that many medical students in less diverse settings do not have. It has given me a lived appreciation of health equity challenges that no textbook can fully convey. Less positive: I have not yet channelled this awareness into active community engagement or advocacy. I remain a learner of social accountability rather than a practitioner of it — a gap I am conscious of and motivated to close.

The UAE's health system is built to handle diversity by design — interpreter services, multicultural staffing, and culturally sensitive protocols are embedded in major hospitals. My awareness of health equity has deepened through both formal education and lived experience, but translating awareness into action requires structural opportunity. As a pre-clinical student, my avenues for meaningful community contribution are limited but not non-existent. The key insight is that social accountability is a posture, not just a skill — it must be cultivated from the very beginning of training.

I should have engaged with GMU's community outreach activities earlier in my first year. Volunteering at public health screening camps, blood donation drives, or health awareness events would have converted my conceptual understanding of social accountability into lived practice. I intend to prioritise these opportunities in the coming academic year and to document my engagement systematically as part of my portfolio.

I will sign up for at least two community health initiatives through GMU in the next semester, focusing on areas where I can contribute most meaningfully given my current skill level — such as health literacy campaigns or diabetes screening awareness. I will also pursue a deeper understanding of the UAE's national health agenda and how the medical curriculum aligns with public health priorities at the national level.

VI · Self & Profession Enhancer

Self & Profession Enhancer

Actively pursues personal and professional development through reflection, feedback, and continuous improvement — demonstrating commitment to lifelong learning.

📓 Reflective Portfolio Practice 🎓 GMU Professional Development Seminars

The act of constructing this e-portfolio — including writing these reflections using the Gibbs Reflective Cycle — represents my most sustained engagement with the Self and Profession Enhancer competency to date. In GMU's professional development sessions during Year 1, I was introduced to reflective practice as a formal educational tool: the idea that structured self-examination is not a supplementary activity but a core clinical skill. I was asked to maintain a learning diary and to revisit it periodically, identifying patterns in my own development.

Initially, structured reflection felt artificial — writing about my feelings in a formal framework seemed forced compared to the spontaneous way one usually processes experience. Over time, and particularly through the process of compiling this portfolio, I have come to appreciate that this discomfort is productive. Committing to articulate what I have learned, what I found difficult, and what I will do differently creates a concrete record of growth that informal reflection cannot match.

The Gibbs Cycle is a practical and genuinely useful framework. Its six stages enforce completeness — it is easy to skip over feelings, or to jump straight to an action plan without analysing why an event unfolded as it did. The structure prevents that intellectual shortcutting. On the less positive side, early reflections I wrote were surface-level, describing events rather than interrogating them. The improvement over subsequent reflections is visible, but the early habit of shallow reflection meant several formative experiences were under-captured.

Shallow early reflections are a normal feature of developing reflective practice. The literature on medical education notes that students typically progress through descriptive reflection toward critical reflection over time, with guided feedback accelerating the process. The GMU curriculum's introduction of reflective frameworks provides the scaffold, but the depth of reflection ultimately depends on the individual's willingness to be honest about uncertainty and error — a psychological openness that itself requires cultivation.

I should have started this portfolio — and this reflective practice — from my first week at GMU rather than retrospectively. The experiences I had during orientation week, my first anatomy session, and the White Coat Ceremony were rich with material for reflection that I did not capture systematically. In future, I will write a brief Gibbs-structured reflection within 48 hours of any significant learning event to preserve the immediacy and emotional honesty of the experience.

I will maintain a digital reflection log — separate from this portfolio — updated after each clinical contact, significant study session, or professional interaction. I will share selected entries with my academic supervisor for feedback and will review the log termly to identify recurring patterns in my development. This portfolio will be updated at the end of each academic year with new evidence and revised reflections as my competence deepens.

VII · Health Systems

Health Systems Proponent

Champions quality improvement, resource stewardship, and evidence-based policy to improve patient care delivery within and across healthcare systems.

🏛️ UAE Health System Orientation 📊 Hospital Administration Observation

During my observership at CKS Hospital, I became aware of how the hospital navigated resource allocation between the catheterisation laboratory and general cardiology wards — a practical health systems challenge playing out in real time. The consultant briefly explained the Dubai Health Authority's regulatory framework governing cardiac procedures, and I observed how insurance authorisations affected the speed of treatment decisions. GMU's orientation modules also introduced us to the UAE's Vision 2031 health objectives and the role of healthcare professionals as stakeholders within a national system.

I felt surprised by how directly administrative and systemic factors shaped bedside clinical decisions. I had not fully considered before the observership that a physician's clinical autonomy is always operating within institutional, regulatory, and economic constraints. This was not discouraging — rather, it sharpened my sense that becoming an effective physician requires understanding the system within which one practices, not just the medicine.

The observership provided authentic exposure to health systems realities that classroom teaching often sanitises. Seeing real insurance authorisation delays and resource allocation decisions made the system tangible rather than theoretical — a significant positive. The limitation was the brevity of the exposure: a few days is insufficient to understand the full systemic picture. I also lacked the background knowledge to ask informed questions about quality improvement processes or patient safety systems in that setting.

Healthcare systems are complex adaptive systems — they cannot be understood through brief observation alone. The UAE's hybrid public-private insurance model creates specific pressures that differ from universal healthcare models, and these structural factors directly shape clinical workflows. Understanding these dynamics is a long-term educational project. My current task is to build sufficient health systems literacy to engage intelligently with these questions when I enter clinical training, and to remain curious about systemic improvement rather than accepting existing workflows as inevitable.

I should have reviewed the DHA regulatory framework and the UAE National Health Strategy before my observership so that I could contextualise what I was seeing. Reading even a brief policy overview would have allowed me to ask more substantive questions and to make more meaningful connections between observable hospital behaviours and their systemic drivers.

I will dedicate time each semester to reading one health policy document or quality improvement case study from the UAE or global health context. I will engage with any GMU electives or seminars that address health systems, healthcare economics, or patient safety, and will seek to understand the quality improvement frameworks used at any hospital I visit during future clinical placements.

VIII · Professionalism

Professionalism

Upholds integrity, accountability, and ethical practice in all professional interactions — demonstrating respect and empathy with patients, colleagues, and the wider medical community.

🥼 White Coat Ceremony — GMU 2024 📜 Professional Pledge

The GMU White Coat Ceremony of 2024 was the most symbolically significant event of my medical education to date. Standing before faculty, family, and peers to receive my white coat and recite the professional pledge made the abstract concept of medical professionalism suddenly concrete. The ceremony was not merely ceremonial — it was an explicit articulation of the ethical contract between a physician-in-training and the profession, and by extension, society. The formal nature of the occasion and the weight placed on it by the institution reinforced for me that professionalism is not an optional attribute but a foundational one.

I felt proud and sobered simultaneously. Pride in having reached this milestone; sobriety in recognising what the commitment entails. The white coat — a symbol I had associated with medicine since childhood — felt heavier when I actually wore it than I had imagined. I was aware of my family watching and of the personal sacrifices and encouragement that had brought me to that room. The experience grounded my motivation in something more durable than ambition: a sense of duty.

The ceremony was effective in establishing professionalism as a core identity, not merely a behavioural standard. The institutional investment in making the event meaningful — the careful choreography, the speeches from senior clinicians, the presence of patients' representatives — all reinforced its importance. A constructive critique is that such ceremonies can feel like a conclusion rather than a beginning: a risk that the professionalism message fades once the immediate emotion subsides. The challenge is sustaining that commitment in the ordinary, less ceremonial moments of daily study and clinical interaction.

Medical professionalism encompasses a wide and sometimes contested set of values: altruism, accountability, excellence, duty, honour, integrity, and respect for others. The white coat ceremony is a rite of passage that serves an important socialisation function — it marks a transition in identity from student to physician-in-training. Research in medical education suggests that professional identity formation is a long-term, iterative process, and that formal ceremonies such as this serve as anchor points in that process. The sustained expression of professionalism comes through consistent small choices — how I treat a struggling classmate, how I respond to critical feedback, how I handle uncertainty.

I could have made a more explicit personal commitment on the day — beyond the shared pledge — by writing a private letter to myself articulating what professionalism means to me specifically, and what I would consider a personal failure of that standard. Such an artefact would serve as a more durable reminder than memory alone. I intend to do this now, retrospectively, and to revisit it at the end of each academic year as a form of professional accountability.

I will write a personal professional values statement this month and attach it to my portfolio as an evidence artefact. I will also engage with any GMU ethics modules, case-based discussions, or professionalism seminars with the explicit goal of testing my values against complex scenarios — recognising that professionalism is often most meaningful under pressure. I will seek role models among senior students and clinicians whose professional conduct I wish to emulate and will reflect on why their behaviour resonates with me.

IX · Evidence Based

Evidence Based Practitioner

Engages in lifelong learning through critical evaluation of research and translates scientific findings into clinical decision-making to advance medical knowledge.

📖 Clinical Literature Reviews — GMU 🔬 Research Methodology Module

In GMU's first-year research methodology module, I was introduced to the hierarchy of evidence — from expert opinion through systematic reviews and randomised controlled trials — and tasked with critically appraising a published clinical paper using a structured checklist. The paper focused on the efficacy of a pharmacological intervention in acute coronary syndrome management, directly relevant to what I had observed during the cardiology observership. The exercise required me to evaluate study design, sample size, outcome measures, bias, and clinical applicability — skills I had not previously applied in a medical context.

I found the process intellectually stimulating but initially overwhelming. Research terminology — p-values, confidence intervals, number needed to treat — required significant background reading before the paper became navigable. Once I established a foundation, the critical appraisal task became genuinely engaging: I began to see clinical decisions not as handed-down wisdom but as provisional conclusions drawn from fallible evidence. This epistemological shift was one of the most significant of my first year, and it felt intellectually liberating.

What worked well was the connection between the paper's clinical subject and my direct observership experience — the contextualisation made the evidence personally relevant and easier to engage with critically. What was challenging was the speed at which the module progressed: there was insufficient time to develop statistical literacy at the depth the task demanded. My final appraisal was competent but thin in its statistical analysis, which I recognise as a current gap in my evidence-based practice skill set.

Evidence-based practice is a discipline that requires both statistical literacy and clinical contextual knowledge — and I am still in the early stages of acquiring both. The gap between encountering a clinical situation (the observership) and being able to critically evaluate the evidence base behind it (the paper appraisal) illustrated the lag inherent in training: clinical exposure often precedes the knowledge needed to fully interrogate it. This is not a flaw in the curriculum — it is the productive tension that drives learning. Recognising this lag helps me direct my self-directed study more intentionally.

I should have invested more time in building statistical literacy independently — using resources such as BMJ's Statistics Notes series or free biostatistics courses — rather than relying solely on the module's pace. For future paper appraisals I will use both a structured checklist and a separate statistical interpretation guide, and I will practise identifying the single most important limitation of each paper rather than listing limitations superficially.

I will complete a self-directed biostatistics primer course before the start of Year 2 and will read and briefly appraise one clinical paper per month in a specialty relevant to my current study or upcoming observerships. I will subscribe to a clinical research journal alert in cardiology and one in general medicine to cultivate the habit of engaging with the primary literature, and will begin to identify potential student research opportunities at GMU to contribute — at however modest a level — to the evidence base myself.
